**Short answer:** Digital nomads in Greece owe no Greek income tax on foreign-sourced income if they stay under 183 days — exceed that threshold and worldwide income becomes taxable as a Greek tax resident.

Greece's Digital Nomad Visa (DNV) is one of the most accessible in Europe — but the tax picture is more nuanced than most guides suggest. Your obligations depend almost entirely on **how long you stay** and **who your clients are**. Here is a plain-English breakdown.

## The Single Most Important Variable: 183 Days

Everything starts here. Under Article 4 of the Greek Income Tax Code (Law 4172/2013), spending **more than 183 days** in Greece within any 12-month period generally makes you a **Greek tax resident** — taxable on your worldwide income.

Below that threshold, if you work exclusively for employers or clients based **outside Greece**, your remote income is generally **not taxed in Greece**.

A few important nuances:
- The 183-day count is cumulative, not consecutive
- Tax residency can also be triggered earlier if Greece is your **permanent home** or your **centre of vital interests** (family, main assets) — even without 183 days
- The official AADE (Greek Tax Authority) rule is 183 days; some government portals reference 180 days — the statutory law says 183

---

## Scenario 1: You Stay Under 183 Days (Short-Stay DNV Holder)

If you hold a Greek DNV and stay fewer than 183 days per year, working remotely for **non-Greek employers or clients**:

- Your foreign-sourced income is **not subject to Greek income tax**
- You are **not required** to file a Greek tax return on that income
- You are still required to have a Greek Tax Number (AFM) to live and transact in Greece

This is the most common situation for true digital nomads using Greece as a temporary base.

---

## Scenario 2: You Stay Over 183 Days (Become a Greek Tax Resident)

Once you cross the 183-day threshold, you become a Greek tax resident and Greece taxes your **worldwide income** at standard progressive rates.

### 2026 Income Tax Brackets (Law 5246/2025)

| Annual Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| €0 – €10,000 | 9% |
| €10,001 – €20,000 | 20% |
| €20,001 – €30,000 | 26% |
| €30,001 – €40,000 | 34% |
| €40,001 – €60,000 | 39% |
| Over €60,000 | 44% |

These are **marginal rates** — you only pay the higher rate on income within each bracket, not on your total income.

Note: Law 5246/2025 reduced mid-bracket rates and raised the 44% threshold from €40,000 to €60,000 for 2026.

## The Article 5C Regime: The "50% Tax Break" Explained

You may have seen references to a 50% tax exemption for digital nomads in Greece. This comes from **Article 5C of Law 4172/2013** (as amended by Law 4916/2022) and is a genuine incentive — but it comes with strict conditions that many DNV holders do not meet.

### Who Qualifies

To benefit from the 50% income exemption for up to **7 years**, you must:

1. Have been a **non-Greek tax resident for 5 of the previous 6 years**
2. Transfer your tax residency **from an EU/EEA country or a country with which Greece has an administrative cooperation agreement**
3. Either:
   - Have an **employment contract with a Greek legal entity** (or a foreign entity with a permanent establishment in Greece), **or**
   - Register as **self-employed in Greece** (issuing Greek invoices)
4. Declare that you intend to remain in Greece for **at least 2 years**

### What It Means in Practice

If you qualify, **50% of your employment or business income is exempt from income tax** for up to 7 years. The effective top rate drops from 44% to 22%.

### The Common Misconception

Article 5C is **not automatically granted** with a Digital Nomad Visa. It applies to people who formally transfer their tax residency to Greece and work for or register a business in Greece. If you hold a DNV but continue working exclusively for foreign clients without registering a Greek business, you likely do not qualify for Article 5C — but you also likely owe no Greek tax if you stay under 183 days.

## If You Have Greek Clients or Register a Business in Greece

If you issue invoices to Greek clients or register as a freelancer in Greece, Greek tax rules apply to that income regardless of your residency status. As a registered freelancer you should be aware of:

- **Progressive income tax** at the brackets above
- **EFKA social security contributions**: approximately €88.66/month for the first 5 years (reduced rate), rising to approximately €217/month at the standard rate
- **Advance tax payments**: 55% of your calculated tax is prepaid in the first 3 years; 100% from year 4 onwards (offset against the following year's final bill)
- **VAT registration** may be required depending on your activity and client base

---

## Double Tax Treaties

If you become a Greek tax resident, a double taxation treaty (DTT) between Greece and your home country may affect where specific types of income are taxed. Greece has treaties with most EU countries, the US, the UK, Canada, and many others.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Under 183 days + foreign clients only** → generally no Greek income tax
- **Over 183 days** → Greek tax resident, worldwide income taxable at standard rates
- **Article 5C** (50% exemption for 7 years) requires formal residency transfer and Greek employment or registered business — it is not automatic with a DNV
- **Greek clients or registered business** → Greek tax obligations apply regardless of days spent
- Tax residency can be triggered by more than just day counts — your centre of life matters too
- **Always get personalised advice** before filing or making residency decisions
