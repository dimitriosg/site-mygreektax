**Short answer:** Greece's Article 5A regime lets qualifying individuals pay a flat €100,000 per year in tax on all foreign-sourced income — regardless of how much that income is. For high earners relocating to Greece, it can represent enormous savings. For most people, it is not the right fit.

Greece does not advertise itself as a tax haven. But since 2019, it has quietly operated one of the most generous non-domicile tax regimes in Europe — one that competes directly with Malta, Portugal's former NHR scheme, and Italy's €100,000 flat tax (which Greece explicitly modelled its own version on). The regime is called Article 5A of the Greek Income Tax Code, and it is specifically designed to attract wealthy individuals to transfer their tax residency to Greece.

This guide explains exactly how it works, who it is aimed at, what the application process looks like, and the honest trade-offs you need to understand before deciding whether to pursue it.

## 1. What is Article 5A?

Article 5A (introduced by Law 4646/2019) is a special tax treatment available to individuals who transfer their tax residency to Greece and have not been Greek tax residents for at least 7 of the previous 8 years. Once approved, it replaces the normal Greek income tax calculation on foreign-sourced income with a single flat annual payment of **€100,000**.

That means whether your foreign income is €200,000 or €5,000,000, you pay the same €100,000 and nothing more on that income in Greece. Greek-sourced income — rent from a Greek property, Greek dividends, Greek employment — is taxed separately under the normal Greek rates.

The regime lasts for a maximum of **15 years**, and it is not automatic. You must apply, qualify, and be approved each year.

## 2. Who is it designed for?

Article 5A is aimed squarely at high-net-worth individuals. The breakeven point — where the flat tax becomes cheaper than normal Greek tax rates — sits roughly around **€350,000–€400,000 of foreign annual income**, depending on the income type and applicable deductions.

Below that level, normal Greek tax residency rules (with treaty protections) will almost always be more tax-efficient. Above it, the savings can be substantial.

The typical Article 5A applicant is one of the following:

- A retired executive or entrepreneur with significant investment income, dividends, or capital gains generated outside Greece
- A business owner who has sold a company and holds the proceeds in a foreign structure
- A family office or UHNWI relocating from a high-tax jurisdiction (UK, Germany, France, Scandinavia)
- A non-EU national seeking a stable EU base with a favourable tax position

It is **not** designed for digital nomads, salaried employees, or freelancers earning under €300,000 — the compliance cost and minimum tax payment alone make it uneconomical at those levels.

## 3. What does the €100,000 cover?

The flat annual payment of €100,000 covers **all foreign-sourced income** — meaning income arising outside Greece. This includes:

- Foreign dividends and interest
- Foreign rental income
- Capital gains on the sale of foreign assets
- Foreign pension income
- Foreign business profits (subject to conditions)

It does **not** cover:
- Income earned in Greece (taxed at normal rates)
- Greek inheritance or gift tax (Article 5A has no estate planning benefits)
- Social insurance contributions (EFKA), if applicable

An important point: Article 5A does **not** automatically eliminate your tax obligations in your home country. If you are a U.S. citizen, U.S. tax obligations follow you regardless of where you live. If you are transferring residency from the UK or Germany, you need to formally break tax residency there — which has its own requirements and timeline. Article 5A solves the Greek side of the equation; your home country situation requires separate advice.

## 4. Family members

Each family member wishing to benefit from the regime must apply separately and pay an additional **€20,000 per person** on top of the primary applicant's €100,000. A couple with two adult children, for example, would pay €100,000 + €20,000 + €20,000 + €20,000 = **€160,000 total** per year.

Minor children are not subject to the additional payment but their income is consolidated with the primary applicant's for the purposes of the flat tax.

## 5. The qualification requirements

To apply for Article 5A, you must:

1. **Not have been a Greek tax resident** in at least 7 of the 8 years immediately preceding the application year
2. **Transfer your tax residency to Greece** — meaning you must actually move, establish your habitual residence here, and register with AADE
3. **Demonstrate investment activity in Greece** — the law requires either a personal investment of at least **€500,000** in Greek real estate, businesses, or securities, or confirmation that you will be making such an investment. This requirement is a significant practical hurdle for many applicants
4. **Apply by 31 March** of the tax year for which you want the regime to apply

The investment threshold is the most commonly misunderstood aspect of Article 5A. It is not a tax — it is a qualifying condition. The €500,000 must be invested in Greece (property purchases are the most common route), not paid to the government.

## 6. How to apply

The application is submitted to AADE and requires:

- A completed application form
- Proof that you were not a Greek tax resident for 7 of the last 8 years (tax certificates from your previous country of residence)
- Evidence of the qualifying investment (or a binding commitment to make one)
- Greek AFM (Tax Identification Number)
- Valid residency documentation

AADE has 60 days to approve or reject the application. In practice, approvals for well-prepared applications typically come through within that window. The regime is then renewable annually — you do not need to reapply from scratch each year, but you do need to confirm continued eligibility and pay the flat tax by the standard Greek tax return deadline.

## 7. What it does not protect you from

Article 5A is sometimes presented as a complete tax solution. It is not. Key limitations:

- **Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC) rules**: If you own a foreign company that earns passive income and is controlled by you, Greek CFC rules may attribute that income to you directly — potentially outside the Article 5A protection
- **Transfer pricing**: Cross-border transactions between you and related entities are subject to Greek transfer pricing rules
- **Exit tax**: If you leave Greece before the 15-year period ends, there is no clawback — but you lose the remaining years of the regime
- **No inheritance tax benefit**: Greece's inheritance tax applies normally to Greek assets. Article 5A does not create any estate planning shield

## 8. Is it worth it?

For the right person, yes — it is one of the most straightforward and legally robust non-dom regimes in Europe. The flat €100,000 is predictable, the 15-year window is long enough to plan around, and Greece offers genuine quality of life alongside it.

The honest checklist:

| Question | If yes, Article 5A may work | If no, probably not |
|---|---|---|
| Is your foreign income above €400k/year? | ✓ | The maths rarely work below this |
| Can you invest €500k in Greece? | ✓ | It's a hard requirement |
| Are you genuinely relocating to Greece? | ✓ | It requires real residency |
| Are you a U.S. citizen? | Get specialist U.S./Greece advice | Standard regime may be simpler |
| Is your income primarily Greek-sourced? | Normal rates apply regardless | Article 5A adds no value here |

If you are in the right bracket and seriously considering a move to Greece, Article 5A deserves a detailed look. The compliance requirements are real but manageable, and the tax savings at the right income level are significant enough to justify the setup cost.

If you are unsure whether you qualify, the best starting point is a consultation with a licensed Greek accountant who can review your specific income sources, your current country's exit requirements, and whether the €500,000 investment threshold is realistic for your situation.
