**Short answer:** To work as a freelancer in Greece you need an AFM, a GEMI registration number, and EFKA social insurance. VAT registration is mandatory once you exceed €10,000 in annual revenue — and sometimes from day one depending on your activity. Tax is calculated on net profit at progressive rates starting at 9%.

Freelancing in Greece is entirely doable, but the registration process trips up almost every expat who tries to do it alone. The steps are spread across three different government agencies, none of which talk to each other particularly well, and the paperwork requirements change depending on your profession. This guide walks through the full process as it stands in 2026.

## What "freelancer" means in Greek tax law

Greece does not have a single legal category called "freelancer." Instead, self-employed individuals fall into one of two categories depending on their activity:

- **Eleftheros Epaggelmatias (ΕΕ)** — liberal professions: lawyers, engineers, accountants, doctors, architects, consultants, IT professionals, designers, and similar knowledge-work roles
- **Atomiki Epicheirisi** — individual business: traders, craftspeople, retail, food, services that are more commercial in nature

The distinction matters because the two categories have different EFKA contribution structures, different presumptive income rules, and different VAT treatment in some cases. If you are unsure which category applies to your work, this is worth clarifying before you register — reclassifying after the fact is possible but creates paperwork.

## Step 1: Get your AFM

If you don't already have a Greek tax identification number (AFM), that comes first. Nothing else can happen without it. You apply at your local tax office (DOY) with your passport and proof of address in Greece.

If you already have an AFM from a previous employment or property ownership in Greece, you use the same number — there is no separate AFM for self-employment.

## Step 2: Register your activity at the tax office

Before you issue a single invoice, you must formally declare the start of your self-employed activity to your DOY. This is called the **Έναρξη Επιτηδεύματος** (start of business declaration). You submit form M2 along with your existing M1 (personal data form if not already on file).

At this stage you also declare:
- Your main activity code (KAD — Κωδικός Αριθμός Δραστηριότητας)
- Whether you are registering for VAT immediately or claiming the small business exemption
- Your accounting method (single or double-entry bookkeeping — most freelancers use single-entry)

The KAD code matters. Choose the one that most accurately describes your work. If you have multiple types of income, you can register multiple KAD codes, but one must be declared as primary.

## Step 3: Register with GEMI

GEMI (General Electronic Commercial Registry) registration is required for individual businesses and for most liberal professions. The registration is done online at [gemi.gr](https://www.gemi.gr) and generates your GEMI number, which you must include on your invoices.

For some liberal professions (doctors, lawyers, engineers) you also need to register with the relevant professional chamber before or alongside GEMI registration. The chamber registration is separate from both the tax office and GEMI.

## Step 4: Register with EFKA

EFKA is Greece's unified social insurance fund. As a self-employed person you are required to register within 30 days of starting your activity. Failing to register on time results in back-contributions being assessed when EFKA eventually discovers the activity — which they will, because EFKA and the tax authority share data.

### EFKA contribution amounts for 2026

Self-employed EFKA contributions are based on a notional income base, not your actual earnings. In 2026 the monthly contributions break down as follows:

| Coverage | Monthly amount |
|---|---|
| Main pension (IKA-ETAM) | €159.04 |
| Supplementary pension (ETEAEP) | €16.98 |
| Healthcare (EOPYY) | €56.42 |
| Lump sum benefit fund | €14.16 |
| **Total** | **€246.60/month** |

New registrants get a **50% discount on the main pension contribution for the first 5 years** of self-employment if they are under 40 at registration. This brings the monthly total to approximately €167 for qualifying individuals.

These figures are updated annually. The amounts above apply from 1 January 2026.

## VAT: when it applies and when it doesn't

### The €10,000 exemption

Freelancers whose annual revenue does not exceed **€10,000** can operate under the small business VAT exemption (Article 39a of the VAT Code). Under this exemption you do not charge VAT on your invoices, do not file VAT returns, and do not receive VAT refunds on your expenses.

If you expect to exceed €10,000 in your first year — or as soon as your cumulative revenue crosses that threshold — you must register for VAT and begin charging it immediately. There is no grace period once the threshold is crossed.

### Standard VAT registration

Once registered for VAT, the standard rate of **24%** applies to most services. Reduced rates apply to specific categories (books, medical services, some food items) — most freelance services fall under the 24% rate.

VAT returns are filed quarterly for most freelancers. You charge 24% on your invoices, collect that money from clients, deduct the VAT you have paid on your own business expenses, and remit the difference to AADE each quarter.

### VAT and foreign clients

If your clients are businesses registered in other EU countries, the **reverse charge mechanism** applies. You issue invoices without VAT and the client accounts for VAT in their own country. You still need to file a recapitulative statement (VIES) listing these transactions. For clients outside the EU, Greek VAT generally does not apply to exported services.

## How freelance income is taxed

Self-employment income in Greece is taxed as business income, not employment income. The progressive tax rates for 2026 are:

| Annual net profit | Tax rate |
|---|---|
| Up to €10,000 | 9% |
| €10,001 – €20,000 | 22% |
| €20,001 – €30,000 | 28% |
| €30,001 – €40,000 | 36% |
| Over €40,000 | 44% |

**Net profit** is your gross revenue minus allowable business expenses. Allowable expenses include professional subscriptions, equipment, software, office costs, and a portion of home office expenses if you work from home.

### The minimum presumptive income

One rule that catches many freelancers off guard: Greece imposes a **minimum presumptive income** for self-employed individuals. Even if your actual net profit is lower, tax is calculated on a minimum of €10,920 per year (2026 figure). If your real profit exceeds this, your real profit is used. If it is lower, the minimum applies — you pay tax as if you earned at least that amount.

### Advance tax payments

Once your first tax return is filed, you will be assessed an **advance payment** equal to 55% of your prior year's tax liability. This advance is credited against the following year's tax bill. For a new freelancer, this means your second year of operation involves paying both the current year's tax and a significant advance for the next — plan your cash flow accordingly.

## Invoicing requirements

All invoices must be issued through **myDATA**, AADE's digital bookkeeping platform. Every invoice you issue is transmitted to myDATA in real time (or at the end of the day at the latest). Your accounting software should handle this automatically — if you are issuing invoices manually without myDATA integration, you are not compliant.

Each invoice must include:
- Your full name and AFM
- Your GEMI number
- The client's name and AFM (or VAT number for foreign clients)
- The KAD activity code
- VAT amount (or "VAT exempt — Article 39a" if under the threshold)
- Sequential invoice number

## Common mistakes to avoid

**Starting work before registering.** Any income earned before your Έναρξη Επιτηδεύματος is treated as undeclared income, not pre-registration activity. Register first, invoice second.

**Ignoring EFKA because you have no income yet.** EFKA contributions are due from the month of registration regardless of whether you have earned anything. They accrue whether you invoice or not.

**Assuming the VAT exemption is permanent.** The €10,000 threshold is cumulative within the calendar year. If you register in October and bill €4,000 in three months, you are fine for that year — but in January the clock resets and you could hit €10,000 by mid-year.

**Not keeping receipts for expenses.** Expenses reduce your taxable profit, but only if they are documented with proper invoices addressed to your business. Personal receipts, cash payments without invoices, and expenses in someone else's name are not deductible.

## What this actually costs in year one

For a freelancer earning €30,000 net in their first year, here is a rough picture of the total tax and contribution burden in 2026:

| Item | Annual amount |
|---|---|
| EFKA contributions (new registrant under 40, 5-year discount) | ~€2,004 |
| EFKA contributions (standard rate) | ~€2,959 |
| Income tax on €30,000 net profit | ~€5,580 |
| Advance tax payment (55% of above) | ~€3,069 |
| **Total cash out in year one (under 40)** | **~€10,653** |
| **Total cash out in year one (standard)** | **~€11,608** |

This is a simplification — actual figures depend on deductible expenses, VAT position, and whether any exemptions apply. But it gives you an order of magnitude to plan around.
